\section{LITERATURE REVIEW}

Indoor air quality (IAQ) monitoring and control has become an essential area of 
research due to its direct impact on health, productivity, and well-being. 
Smith et al.~\cite{smith2020iaq} proposed an IoT-based air quality monitoring 
system using CO$_2$, PM2.5, and VOC sensors, emphasizing real-time data collection 
and visualization. While effective for measurement, their system lacked 
automated environmental control. Lee and Chen~\cite{lee2019aircontrol} 
developed an automated IAQ control system integrated with HVAC units, 
but it was limited to large office environments and relied on expensive 
sensors, reducing accessibility for small-scale applications. Patel and 
Verma~\cite{patel2021pm25} investigated low-cost particulate matter 
sensors for indoor monitoring, highlighting trade-offs between accuracy 
and affordability. Brown~\cite{brown2018iot} provides an overview of IoT 
principles for environmental monitoring, emphasizing modular, scalable designs
 that can be adapted to multiple applications. Additionally, the EPA’s 
 IAQ guidelines~\cite{epa2021iaqguide} stress the importance of continuous
  monitoring and threshold-based alerts for indoor environments, reinforcing 
  the practical need for automated control systems.  

Collectively, these studies indicate that a **cost-effective, 
real-time monitoring system with automated control** is feasible but underrepresented 
in small-scale indoor applications. Our project addresses this gap by combining 
affordable sensors, IoT connectivity, and automated actuation while providing 
real-time alerts and data visualization.
