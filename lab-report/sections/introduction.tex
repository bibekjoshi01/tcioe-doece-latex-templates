\subsection*{Signal Modulation}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Signal Modulation}

Signal Modulation refers to the process of encoding the information/data (baseband signal)
onto a carrier frequency (a high frequency signal) for transmission over a long distance.

The encoding process alters any of the properties of the carrier signal (i.e. amplitude, frequency, or phase) according to the baseband signal that imprints the information onto the carrier signal.

The modulation of baseband signal into high frequency carrier signal provides several benefits:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
    \item Provides better transmission range by shifting the signal to high frequency bands.
    \item Reduces power loss during propagation and allows efficient radiation using practical antennas.
    \item Allows multiple signals to share the same medium using different carrier frequencies (Frequency Division Multiplexing).
    \item Improves signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) over long distances.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Types of Modulation}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Types of Modulation}

Major types of modulations are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\arabic*.}, itemsep=6pt, parsep=12pt, left=0pt]
    \item \textbf{Analog Modulation}

    Analog modulation is a method in which a continuous carrier signal is changed according to a continuous message signal. The message signal varies smoothly with time, such as speech or music. In analog modulation, one property of the carrier wave amplitude, frequency, or phase is varied continuously to represent the information. Common examples include AM and FM radio broadcasting.

    \begin{enumerate}[
            label=\textbf{\alph*.},
            itemsep=6pt,
            parsep=6pt,
            left=0
        ]
        \item \textbf{Amplitude Modulation}  
        It is the process of varying the amplitude of the carrier wave according to the message signal (baseband signal) to modulate the signal.

        \item \textbf{Frequency Modulation}  
        It is the process of varying the frequency of the carrier wave according to the message signal (baseband signal) to modulate the signal.

        \item \textbf{Phase Modulation}  
        It is the process of varying the phase of the carrier wave according to the message signal (baseband signal) to modulate the signal.
    \end{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Digital Modulation}  
    
    Digital modulation is a method in which a carrier signal is changed according to digital data, typically in the form of binary values 0 and 1. Instead of continuous variation, the carrier’s properties change in discrete steps. This type of modulation is widely used in modern communication systems such as mobile communication, Wi-Fi, and digital television.

    \begin{enumerate}[
            label=\textbf{\alph*.},
            itemsep=6pt,
            parsep=6pt,
            left=0
        ]
        \item \textbf{ASK (Amplitude Shift Keying)}  
        It is the process of changing the amplitude of the carrier wave according to the digital data `0' or `1' where the carrier is present when data is `1' and absent when data is `0'.

        \item \textbf{FSK (Frequency Shift Keying)}  
        It is the process of varying the amplitude of carrier waves according to digital data `0' or `1'.

        \item \textbf{PSK (Phase Shift Keying)}  
        It is the process of varying the phase of the carrier wave.
        \begin{enumerate}[label=\roman*., itemsep=0pt, parsep=0pt]
            \item For binary data (1 bit symbol) -- Binary PSK (0 and 180 degrees)
            \item For 2-bit symbols -- Quadrature PSK (4 phase shifts)
        \end{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{QAM (Quadrature Amplitude Modulation)}  
        It is the process that combines both variations in amplitude and phase shift to send multiple bits per symbol increasing the data rate.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Hilbert Transform}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Hilbert Transform}

Hilbert Transform is a mathematical operation used in signal processing that provides a phase shift of $+90^\circ$ to all the negative frequencies and a phase shift of $-90^\circ$ to all the positive frequencies of the signal. It creates a signal that is orthogonal to the original signal.

\subsection*{Amplitude Modulation}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Amplitude Modulation}

Amplitude modulation refers to the process of changing the amplitude of the carrier signal with respect to the baseband or message signal.

Let the carrier signal be: \[
C(t) = A_c \cos(2\pi f_c t)
\]

Modulating signal: \[
m(t) = A_m \sin(2\pi f_m t)
\]

There are various types of Amplitude Modulation:

\subsubsection*{DSB-FC (Double Side Band -- Full Carrier)}

When a signal is amplitude-modulated, the signal is split into two bands i.e. upper band and lower band. If both the upper band and lower band signals are sent along with the carrier signal, it is known as the DSB-FC amplitude modulation.

The basic expression for DSB-FC modulated signal is: 
\[C_m(t) = A_c \left( 1 + k_a m(t) \right) \cos(\omega_c t)\]
\[C_m(t) = A_c \left( 1 + k_a A_m \sin(\omega_m t) \right) \cos(\omega_c t)\]

For proper DSB-FC modulation, the value of $\mu = k_a A_m$ must be less than 1 to preserve the envelope of the message signal.

The DSB-FC modulated signal can be demodulated asynchronously with envelop detection technique.

\textbf{Key Characteristics:}

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
\item In DSB-FC, the amplitude of the carrier is controlled by the message signal.  
\item The information is encoded in the amplitude of the carrier, not in its frequency.  
\item The carrier itself does not carry information, but it helps in simple demodulation, such as envelope detection.  
\item In real systems, AM transmitters are designed with a strong carrier, which ensures that envelope detection works reliably.  
\item The message amplitude is usually limited to prevent distortion and spectral spreading.  
\item The carrier frequency is chosen high enough to allow efficient transmission over long distances and to satisfy antenna size constraints.  
\item The modulation index is typically kept less than 1 to ensure a clean signal and avoid nonlinear distortion.  
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection*{DSB-SC (Double Side Band -- Suppressed Carrier)}

If both the upper sideband and lower sideband signals are transmitted but the carrier signal is suppressed, the modulation is called Double Sideband Suppressed Carrier (DSB-SC) amplitude modulation. In this scheme, the carrier does not carry any information, and all the transmitted power is contained in the sidebands.

The basic expression for a DSB-SC modulated signal is:
\[s(t) = m(t) \cdot c(t)\] where $m(t)$ is the message signal and $c(t)$ is the carrier signal. 
For a single-tone message: 
\[m(t) = A_m \sin(2 \pi f_m t), \quad c(t) = A_c \cos(2 \pi f_c t)\]
Hence, the DSB-SC modulated signal can be expressed as:
\[s(t) = A_m A_c \sin(2 \pi f_m t) \cos(2 \pi f_c t)\]

\textbf{Key Characteristics:}

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
    \item The carrier is suppressed and not transmitted.
    \item Both upper and lower sidebands carry the message information.
    \item Envelope detection cannot be used; coherent detection is required.
    \item Power is more efficiently used compared to DSB-FC since no carrier power is wasted.
    \item The total bandwidth of the DSB-SC signal is $2 f_m$.
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection*{Single Sideband - Suppressed Carrier (SSB-SC)}

If only one of the sidebands (either upper or lower) is transmitted while suppressing the carrier and the other sideband, the modulation scheme is known as Single Sideband Suppressed Carrier (SSB-SC) amplitude modulation. In this method, the transmitted signal occupies minimum bandwidth and utilizes power more efficiently compared to DSB-FC and DSB-SC.

In SSB-SC modulation, the carrier does not carry information and is not transmitted. All the information is contained in a single sideband, either the Upper Sideband (USB) or the Lower Sideband (LSB). As a result, SSB-SC requires coherent detection at the receiver.

\subsubsection*{Methods of SSB-SC Generation}

The commonly used methods for generating SSB-SC signals are:

\textbf{Filter Method (Frequency Discrimination Method)}: In this method, a DSB-SC signal is first generated. A highly selective bandpass filter is then used to remove one of the sidebands while allowing the other sideband to pass. The output of the filter is an SSB-SC signal. This method requires sharp filters, especially when the message frequency is low.

\begin{figure}[h!]

\textbf{Phase Discrimination Method}: In this method, phase shifters are used to introduce a $90^\circ$ phase shift to the message signal and the carrier signal. The resulting signals are combined in such a way that one sideband cancels out due to phase opposition, leaving only the desired sideband. This method avoids the need for sharp filters and is widely used in practical SSB systems.

\subsubsection*{Mathematical Representation}

For a single-tone message signal $m(t) = A_m \sin(2 \pi f_m t)$ and a carrier signal $c(t) = A_c \cos(2 \pi f_c t)$, the SSB-SC signal can be expressed as:

\paragraph{Upper Sideband (USB)}
\[s_{\text{USB}}(t) = \frac{A_m A_c}{2} \cos \left[ 2 \pi (f_c + f_m) t \right]\]

\paragraph{Lower Sideband (LSB)}
\[s_{\text{LSB}}(t) = \frac{A_m A_c}{2} \cos \left[ 2 \pi (f_c - f_m) t \right]\]

\textbf{Key Characteristics:}

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
    \item Only one sideband is transmitted; the carrier and the other sideband are suppressed.
    \item Bandwidth required is $f_m$, which is half that of DSB-FC and DSB-SC.
    \item Highly power efficient since no power is wasted on carrier and redundant sideband.
    \item Requires coherent detection at the receiver.
    \item Widely used in long-distance and bandwidth-constrained communication systems.
\end{itemize}
